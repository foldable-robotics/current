    A01: Mechanism Prototyping

    Introduction

    The purpose of this assignment is to develop your knowledge and understanding of rapid prototyping techniques and the kinematics of folded systems

    Instructions

    Part 1: Popup Book (Due in class Monday, April 29)

    1. Read the entire assignment before starting.
    2. Read this paper[1]. Look through other online sources you can find, including the book by Birmingham [2], or google.
    3. From these initial sources, try to learn more about the mechanisms using Google Scholar.
    4. Make a table of three different origami-inspired or popup mechanisms used in research, including an image, URL, citation reference (eg [1]), and short description. (All citations must be included in your references section.) This should be submitted with your final submission, but it does not need to be brought to class.
    5. Make a popup greeting card that uses the opening and closing of the card to "erect" the mechanism. In other words, when you open the book, your mechanism unfolds, revealing its final shape.
      • the card should be composed of at least two different mechanism types and three total sub-mechanisms.
      • Connect at least two of your mechanisms together in series.
      • Connect at least two of your mechanisms together in parallel.
      • You should use at least one planar and one spherical mechanism in your popup book. Identify which is which. If you don't know the difference, ask in the forum on Canvas
      • Focus on demonstrating your knowledge of the mechanisms you found, rather than showing complex tesselations or repeating patterns of the same thing.

    1. Bring to class on Monday, April 29. The remainder of the assignment is due by the date indicated on Canvas.

    Part 2: Document your Prototype

    1. Take 3 photos of your final popup book in open, closed, and midway positions. The simple example from above is shown in those three states, for example. Over-draw each joint in a vector drawing program to highlight joint locations in the image.

    2. Take a video of your popup book opening and closing, and post to YouTube.

    3. Include the table created above in your final report

    Part 3: Planar Mechanism

    1. Make a simple, unique, planar, four-bar linkage using stiff paper, cardstock, or cardboard. None of the sides of this linkage should be the same length. Remember, a planar mechanism is a mechanism whose motion can be fully captured by a planar cross section of the device, as seen below:

      Note: There is no requirement to reinforce or stiffen the device for the purposes of this assignment, as long as the cardstock you use is stiff enough to not bend noticeably in your photos.

    2. Document your physical prototype.

      1. Label each of the physical links as link A, link B, link C, and link D.
      2. Measure and note the length of each link.
      3. Take pictures of your device from the top, side, and other oblique angles, ensuring that link labels are visible.
      4. Take pictures of your device from the side at both limits of its range of motion, including at least two intermediate configurations. You will be performing your analysis at these configurations.

      5. Measure the angles of each joint in each configuration, using the photos you took (from the side) to identify each of your mechanism's joint angles and collect them in a table. You will use these values in part 3.

        • You can use any software such as photoshop, GIMP, inkscape, solidworks, etc to measure angles.
        • It is not necessary to recreate the linkage using a vector-drawing program.

        Please ensure consistency in the definition of your angles and rotations and in the values you measure. Define your incremental rotation angle to be considered positive when it is defines the angle from the proximal link to the distal link, with the axis pointing out of the page, using the right hand rule.

      6. Measure the range of motion of your device. How far can you rotate the input in the positive and negative direction without bending any links or having links physically interfere with each other? How far does your output move? At what points or between what links does your device interfere with itself first? Justify your measurements with the photos taken previously.

    3. Create a diagram of your four-bar linkage in the plane in one of its intermediate configurations, ensuring consistency with the physical prototype.

      1. Define the base link. Identify the "grounded" base link of the device as a reference frame for defining the moving distal links. The simplest and most straightforward way to think of the base link is the link that is permanently attached to the floor or table.
      2. Define the input. You will need one input link (per the device's degrees of freedom) to fully define the position of the output. The input link should be adjacent to the base link, and the angle defined between the base and input link should be considered the "input angle".
      3. Define the output of the mechanism by selecting a point \(p_{out}\) on a different link. This output point might be a point of interest for your popup-book, for example.
      4. Identify the relative rotation angles between neighboring frames.
      5. Label each of the physical links as link A, link B, link C, and link D.
      6. Label the frames on each link. Each link should be considered to contain its own rigid reference frame. Draw a pair of orthogonal unit vectors on the plane of the link and identify these vectors (eg \(\hat{a}_x\), \(\hat{a}_y\), \(\hat{b}_z\)) on the links

    Part 4: Code Implementation

    In this section you will compose the transforms and kinematics for your mechanism and plot it using the resulting vectors

    1. Create a new jupyter/colab notebook. In the first cell, paste in the kinematics code you began in class for defining rotations and/or quaternions.
    2. Define the appropriate 3D local rotational transform for each link, describing the rotation between the closest proximal reference frame and its own.
      • Use a Python function to create quaternions or rotation matrices with a rotation axis and joint angle you supply.
      • For the joint axis and joint angle, use the axis you defined earlier, and the angle you measured in previous steps, to ensure consistency between your description and your math.
    3. Compose the 3D global rotational transform for each link's reference frame from the base frame you previously defined.

    4. Define the 3D vectors that describe the vector beetween two points on each link, the first point being a point located on / along the proximal joint axis, the second being a point located on / along the distal joint axis, using the unit vectors of the local reference frame that are attached to that link. For the output link, create the local vector that points to \(p_{out}\)

    5. Define the two global 3D vectors that describe the position of the end effector position (\(p_{out_1}\) and \(p_{out_2}\) in my drawing) from the origin (0,0,0) along each of the two possible paths, using only base frame unit vectors.

      It is reasonable to describe two separate chains to the same point, and to supply successive rotations to each. These two points may not actually touch due to measurement error.

    6. Compute the "error" between your two calculations for \(p_{out}\), using the definition for length of a vector.

    7. Using matplotlib, plot the mechanism in its computed position along both paths, from proximal to distal. Do not plot the gap between the same point via separate open loop chains.

      You may either use a 2D or 3D plot, but the computations leading up to this should be in 3D, and consistent with the device prototyped above.

    Part 5: Discussion

    Please discuss each of the following points. Your answer should be a short passage consisting of multiple sentences.

    1. Comment on how well your mechanism as plotted in part 3 matched the device you prototyped in part 2.
    2. Identify potential pitfalls or sources of error that could lead to differences between the two.

    What Worked Well Last year

    • Being Precise
      • If you cut by hand, use a straightedge and x-acto.
      • Consider using a laser cutter to score sheet or weaken.
    • Making things out of one piece
    • Making hinges with flexible materials
      • Consider taping or reinforcing joints.
    • Making links with stiff materials
    • Scoring hinges
    • Practicing

    What didn't work well last year

    • flaps & slides (they catch and jam)
    • small features
    • overlapping parts / features
    • corrugated cardboard
    • misalignments due to assembly
    • your first try
    • hysteresis
    • superglue
